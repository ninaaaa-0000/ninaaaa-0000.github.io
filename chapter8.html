8.1 Physical security


Health aspects


Health and safety regulations advise that all computer systems have at least tiltable and anti-glare screens,
adjustable chairs and foot supports, suitable lighting and uncluttered work stations,
and recommend frequent breaks and frequent eye sets.
Here I list a number of health risks as well as giving some idea of how each risk can be removed or minimised.


Posture-related injuries from computer use


  1. Back and neck pain
  2. Headaches
  3. shoulder and arm pain

Eyestrain from computer use


  1. Focusing your eyes at the same distance point for long periods of time causes fatigue.
  2. The human eye structurally prefers to look at objects more than six metres away, so any work performed close up puts extra demands on your eye muscles.

Injuries from laptop computers


  1. The growing use of laptop computers has caused more pains, strains and injuries among computer users.
  2. Laptop computers were designed to be used for short periods of time. But these days many people use a laptop all the time.
  3. Carrying your laptop around can also strain your muscles and joints

Children and computer-related injuries


  1. Children might also get overuse injuries of the hand, and muscle and joint problems such as back and neck pain or headaches.
  2. Some research has shown that playing violent computer games and a large amount of game time may cause aggressive behaviour in some children and may negatively affect a childâ€™s school work.

Preventing computer-related muscle and joint injuries


  1. Sit at an adjustable desk specially designed for use with computers.
  2. Have the computer monitor (screen) either at eye level or slightly lower.
  3. Adjust your chair so that your feet rest flat on the floor, or use a footstool.

Preventing eyestrain from computer use


  1. Tilt the screen slightly to avoid reflections or glare.
  2. Make sure the screen is not too close to your face.
  3. Put the screen either at eye level or slightly lower.

Preventing injury from laptop computers


  1. Use a correctly set-up desktop computer instead of a laptop as often as you can.
  2. Use peripheral equipment, such as a docking station, separate keyboard, mouse and laptop stand.
  3. Take frequent breaks.

Health risks from computer games


  1. Overuse injuries of the hand
  2. Obesity
  3. Muscle and joint problems
  4. Eyestrain
  5. Behavioural problems including aggressive behaviour



Safety aspects


Safety is a different issue to health, it is more concerned with dangers that could lead to serious injoury or even loss of life.
Some of the more common examples of safety risks, together with possible solutions, are list here.


Trailing Cables


  1. Computer equipment is often connected to lots ofcables: power, network, etc.
  2. If these cables are laying on the floor, they can cause people to trip over them.
  3. Solution: Place cables inside cable ducts, orunder the carpet / flooring

Spilt Drinks or Food


  1. If any liquids are spilt on electrical equipment, such a s a computer, it can result in damage to the equipment, or an electric shock to the user.
  2. Solution: Keep drinks and food away from computers

Overloaded Power Sockets


  1. Plugging too many power cables into a socket can result in the socket being overloaded, overheating, and a fire starting.
  2. Solution: Never plug too many cables into a socket. Always make sure there are fire extinguishers nearby

Heavy Objects Falling


  1. Many items of computer equipment are very heavy: CRT monitors, laser printers, etc. Heavy items can cause serious injury if they fall on people.
  2. Solution: Make sure equipment is placed on strong tables / shelves












8.2 E-Safety


What does E-safety mean?


E-Safety at a simple level means being safe on the internet. Some people also include the safe use of technology in this as well.
The pace at which technology is evolving can make it difficult to know what to include when talking about the safe use of the internet.


It refers to safety when using the internet.
i.e., keeping personal data safe and applies to any of the following devices:

Mobile phone

Computer or tablet

Game console

Wireless technology


Personal data refers to any data concerning a living persom who can be identified either from the data itself or from the data in conjunction with other information.

Note 1

Examples of persomal data include

  • Name
  • Address
  • Date of birth
  • Medical history
  • Banking details

Note 2

Some persomal data is often referred to as sensitive personal data and includes

  • Ethnic origin
  • Personal views
  • Religion
  • Sexual orientation
  • Criminal activity

E-Safety also refers to the benefits, risks and responsibilities when using ICT.
The following list is by no means exhaustive but gives some idea of the e-safety issues that can be encountered by users of ICT hardware:


  • Don't give our any personal information to people who are unknown to you
  • Don't send people photos of yourself
  • Only use websites recommended by teachers and only use learner-friendly search engine
  • Only email people you know
  • Only open emails from known sources

    Effective security of data


    Define the term hacking and describe its effects


    Definition: Hacking is an attempt to exploit a computer system or a private network inside a computer.
    Simply put, it is the unauthorised access to or control over computer network security systems for some illicit purpose.
    They can destroy, steal or even prevent authorized users from accessing the system.




    Phishing


    The creator sends out legitimate-looking emails to target users. As soon as the recipient clicks on a link in the email or attachment, they are sent to a fake website or they are fooled into giving personal data in replying to the email. The email often appears to come from a trusted source, such as bank or well-known service provider.

    1. The creator of the email can gain personal data. such as bank account data or credit card numbers, from the user.

    2. This can lead to fraud or identity theft.

    1. Many ISPs web browsers filter out phishing emails.

    2. Usere should always be cautions when opening emails or attachments.

    3. Don't click on executable attachments that end in .exe, .bat, .com or .php, for example.

    Add all these above = Phishing




    Pharming


    This is malicious code installed on a user's computer or on a web server, the code will redirect the user to a fake website without their knowledge (the user doesn't have to take any action, unlike phishing).

    1. The creator of the malicious code can gain personal data such as credit/debit card details from users when they visit the fake website; usually the website appears to be that of a well-known and trusted company.

    2. Pharming can lead to fraud or identity theft.

    1. Some anti-spyware software can identify and remove pharming code from a user's computer.

    2. The user should always be alert and look out for clues that they are being redirected to another website.

    Add all these above = Pharming




    Viruses


    This program code or software that can replicate/copy itself with the intention of deleting or corrupting files on a computer; they often cause the computer to malfunction (for example, by filling up the hard drive with data).

    1. Viruses can cause the computer to 'crash', stop functioning normally or become unresponsive.

    2. The software can delete files or data on a computer.

    3. The software can corrupt operating system files, making the computer run slowly or even 'crash'.

    1. Install anti-virus software and updata it regularly.

    2. Don't use software from unknown sources.

    3. Be careful when opening emails or attachments from unknown senders.

    Add all these above = Viruses
